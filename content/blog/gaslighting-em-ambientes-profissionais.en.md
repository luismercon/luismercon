The term "gaslighting" comes from the 1938 play "Gas Light", written by Patrick Hamilton, and the 1944 film directed by George Cukor. In the story, a husband manipulates his surroundings to make his wife question her sanity. Originating in fiction, the concept carries a Manichean bias, depicting a clear villain (the husband) and a victim (the wife).

Yet, applying gaslighting to real-life situations, it's naive to assume that such damaging psychological effects always stem from the intentionally harmful actions of a so-called villain.

A key aspect to understand is the ambiguity of "gaslighting", referring to two distinct scenarios. One involves an individual's behavior; the other, the psychological impact on one or more persons.

These aspects can exist independently. Someone might exhibit manipulative behavior without effectively inducing typical gaslighting effects. Conversely, these effects might manifest in people even in the absence of malicious or deliberate conduct.

Consider this within a workplace context.

Unlike in fiction, real-world businesses don't always feature easily identifiable malicious intent. Yet, it's common to encounter people questioning their sanity, known as the gaslighting **effect**. Hostile environments, poor communication, and inexperienced or insecure leaders can create a perfect storm, leading to gaslighting effects among employees.

Imagine an employee criticized for not completing a task as the leadership perceives as correct. When the employee insists the task was done as instructed, the leader retorts, "But it was implied!"

Frequent occurrences of such situations can lead employees to doubt their capabilities, as their efforts are routinely critiqued and deemed incorrect. This results in a palpable gaslighting **effect** on the employee's psyche.

However, it's also worth considering that the leader might not intend to harm or persecute. The issue isn't villainy, but poor leadership and ineffective communication. Let's face it, incompetent leaders are not rare in the job market.

Thus, viewing scenarios as always having malicious leaders and victimized employees, or vice versa, is overly simplistic.

In the real world, people can inadvertently cause harm, even without malicious intent. When such behavior emanates from leadership in the workplace, it indicates a major organizational problem.

Behaviors and intentions are often nuanced and ambiguous. Effective communication is the key to resolving such issues, being a pivotal skill for any team leader, arguably more crucial than technical expertise.

If you are a leader, consider these recommendations:

1. Be explicit in your demands; don't assume actions are implicit.

2. Avoid relying solely on what seems obvious. Reiteration is preferable to risking information omission.

3. Take responsibility for clear communication. If an employee misunderstands, consider the lapse in clarity as your oversight.

4. Spend time clearly articulating what needs to be done and the specific way it should be carried out. Proficiency in writing enhances effective communication.

5. Be aware that communication gaps allow for interpretive freedom in task execution, often lacking your context.

6. Practice empathy; understand the executor's perspective before critiquing or requesting redoing tasks. Communicate compassionately and manage your stress to prevent affecting the entire team.
