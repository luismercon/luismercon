This is not a script. It's not a manual. Don't take it as a step-by-step of how to change careers. I don't mean to teach anything. It's just the story of how I changed careers and became a programmer, after a 13-year career as a lawyer.

## The Law

I graduated in 2007 from a good law school and I passed the Bar Exam in the same year. In the legal area, I did a lot: I practice law; did a master's degree; published a book; worked in large and small law markets; in public agencies and in the private sector. And, like almost everyone who no longer wants to be a lawyer in Brazil, I've studied hard to try to get a public office (and I didn't succeed).

I like Law, but there came a time in my life when changing professions was my best option. The world has changed and so did I. But every change is within a context, which cannot be disregarded.

## The Context

In 2019, my family and I decided not to live in Brazil anymore and we took on the challenges of being immigrants. The willingness was always there existed, but there was no concrete plan. At that time, I still felt that I should insist on some plans in the legal area. But in 2017, when my daughter was born, I had a very strong feeling that we would soon have to decide if we were going to make it happen.

My wife and I worked in the public sector at that time. In one way or another, any public agency in Brazil is influenced by politics. Those who are not tenured, even if with a technical profile (as was my case), are exposed to storms that happen every two years, which in Brazil we call elections.

During the 2018 election campaign, we went through a specific situation regarding my wife's position, which caused us immense insecurity. We managed to get around the problem in time, but the scare was enough for us to decide that it was time to pack our bags.

To make such a sudden change I needed an excuse. I needed to give myself a reason, a goal, and a direction, even if the path had to be adjusted in the future. I didn't want to tell everyone that I was just leaving without any explanation.

As I already had a master's degree, the excuse I found was a doctorate program in law. It made sense that the destination was Portugal, as I didn't need to do a lot of planning to apply. I wouldn't have to worry about English proficiency exams and document translations. I just applied and was easily approved at the University of Coimbra. It was a fast track.

As soon as the decision to move was taken, I tried to organize myself professionally and financially to make it possible. I was fortunate of getting a remote job as a lawyer in one of the firms I had previously worked for. We're talking about before the pandemic when remote work was an exception. The mindset towards remote work has completely changed after COVID-19. However, the pandemic shook the economy, and I lost my remote job.

## The Decision

In the situation I was in, common sense would be to return to Brazil or try a career as a lawyer in Portugal. The first was something I didn't even consider. The latter was something I considered. I even joined the Bar Association and thought about possible niches in Portugal. But, deep down, something told me that it would take a long time to settle up myself professionally in a foreign country as a lawyer.

It was, in fact, a reset. This perception was confirmed each time I talked to Brazilian colleagues who were already practicing law in Portugal. Besides, I no longer wanted to experience the fragility and instability of a self-employed person. I wanted a job that paid in euros.

Then I realized that the time it would take for me to (re)build a career as a lawyer in Portugal would be as long as changing professions completely. I was faced with an opportunity to change my career path. Then, in a conversation with friends, I found out about a professional reskilling program for the IT area at the University of Coimbra.

That was the opportunity I was looking for.

I don't want to say too much about the course. Just know that it was a high investment that would require me a year of hard work. A high stakes, with a worthy reward. If everything went well and I managed to finish the course, I would get a job as an intern at one of the university's partner companies.

For me, it was the best way to start a new career. Not to mention that the salary for interns in IT is better than most jobs in Portugal.

## The Change

It wasn't an easy decision. Human beings like routine. Our cognition is formed when we establish repeating patterns. But in a world that changes constantly (and ever faster!), learning to change is a huge advantage.

In an interview given at an event promoted by Google, historian Yuval Harari was asked about the future, and what we should teach children. His response was something like this:

> (...) nobody knows what the world will be like in 2050, except that it will be very different. So the things that should be emphasized the most in education are things like emotional intelligence and mental stability. These are things they will need for sure. One skill that will be needed is the ability to reinvent yourself over and over again throughout your life. From an age, it is very difficult to reinvent yourself. So it will be necessary to build identities no longer as stone buildings, but as tents, which can be folded and taken elsewhere. You don't know where you're going to move, but you're going to move.

I had already watched this interview before I lost my job. I've rewatched it a few times since then and that point always leads me to a lot of thoughts. The idea of being able to reinvent yourself is something that makes a lot of sense to me, and I was living this need when I lost my job.

That's how I signed up for the Java programming course admission process.

About the ability to reinvent oneself, it is worth telling something curious that happened to me in the admission process for the programming course. In the interview, which was led by an HR  specialist and the program coordinator, I was asked the question of where I saw myself in five years.

My answer was sincere and straightforward. I said I didn't know and I try not to worry about it. When I think about where I was five years ago, it was unimaginable that I would be an immigrant changing career today. It would be very naive to think about that five-year future because many things can happen. There will be many factors that are far from my control (like a pandemic, for example), and it doesn't make sense to draw up a very concrete plan for the long term.

But while I was answering that, I remembered Harari, and I added to my answer by saying that if in five years I needed to change careers again, at least I would have some experience in change.

Not sure if they liked my answer. I had the feeling that the interviewer was surprised. Perhaps she had done too many interviews with young people full of dreams. But, changing careers is such a delicate matter, especially in my case: a 38 years old guy with a professional career.

## One Career?

When talking about my career change, it's common for people to think that I had a lot of courage. But I think it's not a matter of having or not having the courage. It's easy to be brave when there's little to lose.

My biggest challenge was dealing with the feeling of having spent a lot of my life on a legal education that I would no longer follow as a profession.

I was raised in a social context where it made sense to have a career and dedicate yourself for the rest of your life. This is a view that is deeply rooted in the generations of our parents and grandparents. The profession chosen when we are a teenager becomes a part of our identity. For some people, it may be difficult to recognize themselves outside of it.

There is an episode of the Boa Noite Internet podcast, presented by Cris Dias, which is called "You are not your badge", and which deals with this theme in a very interesting way (by the way, I recommend all the episodes of this podcast! Only in Portuguese...).

Therefore, my biggest challenge was knowing how to deal with self-judgment and the feeling of wasted time, associated with the looks that I failed professionally. Yep, those looks are real. Even if we shouldn't care about it, it affects us. No one wants to be seen as a loser.

I don't know if you've noticed, but in the world we live in, many people wish "success" when someone has a birthday. This fascination with success, and disapproval of failure, created a new kind of hell.

That's why I had to reframe what success is in my mind. For me, **success in the 21st century is having the ability to reinvent yourself during your lifetime.**

## A new beginning

In 2020, reinventing myself professionally was my best option. I'm convinced it was the best I could do. If common sense were to follow, perhaps I should have changed my life to suit my professional career as a lawyer, which was something that chose when I was just 18.

But, I don't want to be stuck with a choice made such a long time ago, when the world was completely different. I consider my life more important than a professional career. So for me, it makes more sense to switch careers and become a programmer, to fit the life I want for myself and my family.
